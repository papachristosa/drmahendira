Osteoarthritis Clinic · Condition guide

Trigger finger

A finger that catches, clicks, or locks when you bend or straighten it.

Back to the Osteoarthritis ClinicAll patient guides

What it is

Trigger finger happens when the tendon that bends a finger cannot glide smoothly through its sheath. The finger can click, catch, or lock in a bent position, sometimes with a tender lump in the palm. It is more common with diabetes.

Common symptoms

  • Clicking or catching as you move the finger
  • A finger that locks bent and needs easing straight
  • A tender bump at the base of the finger in the palm
  • Stiffness, often worse in the morning

What can help

  • Resting from aggravating gripping, with gentle movement
  • A night splint to rest the finger
  • Simple pain relief

Where an injection fits

A corticosteroid injection into the tendon sheath is the usual first treatment and works well for many people. Relief often appears within about a week, and roughly half to two-thirds of fingers settle with one or two injections. If a finger keeps locking after injections, a small day-surgery procedure to release the tendon is highly effective.
