Osteoarthritis Clinic · Patient guide

After your injection

Simple advice for the day of your injection and the days after, whichever injection you had.

Back to the Osteoarthritis ClinicAll patient guides

Right after the injection

If we used a local anaesthetic, the area may feel numb for a few hours, and part of the early pain relief comes from that. Take care with a numb joint until normal feeling returns. If you feel faint or unwell straight after, sit or lie down and let us know before you leave.

The first day or two

  • Rest the joint or area and avoid hard or repetitive use for a day or two.
  • An ice pack for 10 to 15 minutes can help soreness. Put a thin cloth between the ice and your skin.
  • Simple pain relief such as acetaminophen is usually fine. Ask us if you are unsure.
  • Avoid anti-inflammatory tablets (NSAIDs) for the first 24 hours unless your provider has told you otherwise.
  • You can normally do light daily activities and gentle movement.
  • Keep any small dressing on for a few hours, then keep the site clean and dry.

What is normal

  • With a steroid injection, a short flare of increased pain for a day or two is common before it settles.
  • Steroids can raise blood sugar for a few days. If you have diabetes, check your levels more often and treat as usual.
  • A little bruising or tenderness at the injection site can happen.

When you should feel a difference

A steroid injection often starts to help within a few days. Viscosupplementation with Durolane usually works more slowly, building over a few weeks. Injections tend to work best when you pair them with exercise, weight management, or the therapy your team has suggested, rather than on their own.

Follow-up

We will let you know whether a review is needed and when. If your symptoms are not improving as expected, or they come back, contact the clinic so we can plan the next step.

!When to seek urgent care. Call the clinic or seek care if the joint or area becomes hot, very swollen, or increasingly painful over the days after the injection, if redness spreads, or if you develop a fever above 38°C (100.4°F) or feel generally unwell. These can be signs of infection, which is rare but needs prompt attention.
