Travelling abroad, live vaccines, and yellow fever

If you take medicines that calm your immune system, a few travel vaccines need extra thought before you go abroad. The one to plan around is yellow fever, because it is a live vaccine that is often not safe on these medicines, and some countries ask for proof of it before they let you in. The good news is that most travel vaccines are not live and are perfectly fine. This page explains the difference, how to check your own trip, and what to do if you cannot have a vaccine a country asks for.

VaccinationsAll patient guides
iPlan early. If you are on immune-calming treatment and thinking about international travel, see a travel health clinic about six weeks before you go, and tell your rheumatology team. Leaving it late can mean a vaccine cannot be given in time, or a trip has to change.

1. Why this matters for you

Many arthritis medicines work by calming the immune system. Most vaccines are made from a germ that has been killed or taken apart, so there is nothing living in them; these are called non-live, or inactivated, vaccines, and they are safe to have while you are on your treatment. A smaller group of vaccines are made from a living but weakened germ. These live vaccines can occasionally cause an infection in someone whose immune system is being suppressed, so they are usually avoided or carefully timed. For travel, the live vaccine that matters most is yellow fever, partly because of your medicines and partly because it is sometimes required by law to enter a country.

3. Yellow fever: the one to plan around

Yellow fever is a serious infection spread by mosquitoes in parts of sub-Saharan Africa and in tropical South America, around the Amazon basin. The vaccine is a live vaccine, and for most people on immune-calming treatment it is not considered safe to give. That creates two separate questions for a trip: is there a real risk of yellow fever where you are going, and does any country on your route require proof of vaccination to let you in.

Those two things are not the same. Some countries ask every traveller for proof. Others ask for it only if you have recently been in, or even just passed through, a country where yellow fever occurs, so a connecting flight can matter. Requirements also change, so an old list or a friend's past trip is not a safe guide.

4. How to check your own trip

The most reliable way to check, for Canadians, is the Government of Canada travel advice for your destination. For every country on your route, including places you only connect through, open its Travel Advice and Advisories page, go to the Health tab, and look under pre-travel vaccinations. If anything is unusual, the destination's embassy or consulate in Canada can confirm its current entry rules. Do this well before you book, and take the results to your travel clinic.

5. If you cannot have a vaccine a country asks for

Being unable to have the yellow fever vaccine does not automatically mean you cannot travel. A doctor at a designated yellow fever centre can record a medical exemption on the official certificate, called the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis. Many countries accept this exemption in place of the vaccine, although acceptance is not guaranteed everywhere, so it needs checking for your specific route.

There is a second point that matters just as much. A medical exemption removes the paperwork barrier, but it does not protect you from the infection. If your trip genuinely goes into a yellow fever area, and you cannot be protected by the vaccine, that is worth an honest conversation with your travel clinic and your rheumatology team about whether, and how, to go.

6. Where to go, and yellow fever centres near the clinic

In Canada, the yellow fever vaccine and its certificate, including a medical exemption, are only available at vaccination centres designated by the Public Health Agency of Canada. These centres also provide wider travel health advice. The centres below are designated sites in downtown Toronto, near St. Michael's Hospital. 7. A short checklist before you book

1

Tell your rheumatology team

Let them know your travel plans early, especially before any live or travel vaccine, or if you are about to start a new medicine.

2

See a travel clinic early

About six weeks before departure, so there is time for advice, any vaccines you can safely have, and paperwork.

3

Check every country on your route

Including connections, using the Government of Canada travel advice Health tab.

4

Carry your medicine list

Bring an up-to-date list of your medicines and doses to your travel appointment and on your trip.
