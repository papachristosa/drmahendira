5 Complete Vegetarian Meals

Anti-Inflammatory Recipes for RA. By Dr. D. Mahendira.

All nutrition guidesAll patient guides

A growing body of evidence supports the role of an anti-inflammatory diet in managing rheumatoid arthritis. Plant-based and vegetarian diets are associated with lower levels of C-reactive protein (CRP), reduced disease activity scores, and improved patient-reported outcomes. These five complete vegetarian meals are designed to be easy to prepare, rich in anti-inflammatory nutrients, and adapted for patients who may experience joint stiffness or limited hand function.

Each recipe includes a full ingredient list, step-by-step instructions, an explanation of its anti-inflammatory mechanism, and a nutritional summary. All meals provide a complete balance of macronutrients suitable as a main dish.

1

Turmeric & Red Lentil Stew with Wilted Greens

30 minutes | 4 servings | Easy, One Pot | ~380 kcal per serving

This warming one-pot stew is built around red lentils, one of the most nutrient-dense vegetarian protein sources available. The combination of turmeric, ginger, and black pepper creates a synergistic anti-inflammatory effect, while the lentils provide both complete plant protein and prebiotic fibre that supports gut microbial diversity, a key factor in immune regulation for RA patients.

Why it helps: Curcumin in turmeric inhibits the NF-κB and COX-2 inflammatory pathways that drive joint destruction in RA. Black pepper increases curcumin bioavailability by up to 2,000%. Red lentils provide folate (important for patients on methotrexate) and resistant starch that feeds anti-inflammatory gut bacteria. Dark leafy greens deliver magnesium, vitamin K, and antioxidant carotenoids.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup dried red lentils, rinsed
  • 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, grated
  • 1½ tsp ground turmeric
  • ½ tsp black pepper
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 can (400 ml) light coconut milk
  • 2 cups vegetable broth
  • 1 can (400 g) diced tomatoes
  • 4 large handfuls spinach or kale, roughly chopped
  • Juice of ½ lemon
  • Salt to taste

Method

  1. Heat the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the diced onion and cook for 3 to 4 minutes until softened and translucent.
  2. Add the garlic, ginger, turmeric, black pepper, and cumin. Stir for 1 minute until fragrant, being careful not to burn the garlic.
  3. Add the rinsed red lentils, diced tomatoes, coconut milk, and vegetable broth. Stir to combine everything evenly.
  4. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce the heat to low. Cover and simmer for 18 to 20 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the lentils are completely soft and beginning to break down.
  5. Add the chopped spinach or kale. Stir and cook for 2 to 3 minutes until the greens are wilted.
  6. Remove from heat. Stir in the lemon juice and season with salt. Serve warm in bowls.

Tip. This stew stores well in the refrigerator for up to 4 days and freezes for up to 3 months, making it ideal for batch cooking on low-symptom days. The texture thickens as it cools, add a splash of broth when reheating.

Nutrition (per serving). Protein: 18 g | Fibre: 14 g | Omega-6:Omega-3 ratio: favourable | Iron: 6 mg | Folate: 180 µg

2

Mediterranean Chickpea & Roasted Vegetable Bowl

35 minutes | 2 servings | Easy, One Tray + Assembly | ~450 kcal per serving

The Mediterranean dietary pattern has the strongest evidence base of any diet for reducing inflammation in rheumatoid arthritis. This bowl brings together the core elements of that pattern: extra-virgin olive oil, legumes, whole grains, and abundant vegetables. Roasting the vegetables caramelises their natural sugars and concentrates their antioxidant content.

Why it helps: Extra-virgin olive oil contains oleocanthal, which has a mechanism of action similar to ibuprofen, inhibiting COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes. Chickpeas provide saponins and isoflavones with documented anti-inflammatory activity. Roasted bell peppers are among the richest dietary sources of vitamin C, which supports collagen synthesis in damaged joint cartilage. The tahini dressing provides sesamin, a lignan with demonstrated immunomodulatory properties.

Ingredients

  • 1 can (400 g) chickpeas, drained, rinsed, and patted dry
  • 1 large red bell pepper, cut into strips
  • 1 medium courgette (zucchini), sliced into half-moons
  • 1 small red onion, cut into wedges
  • 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil, divided
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • ½ tsp ground cumin
  • 1½ cups cooked quinoa or brown rice
  • 2 large handfuls rocket (arugula) or mixed greens
  • 2 tbsp tahini
  • Juice of 1 lemon
  • 1 clove garlic, finely grated
  • 2 tbsp water
  • Salt, pepper, and fresh parsley to garnish

Method

  1. Preheat the oven to 210°C (410°F). Line a large baking tray with parchment paper.
  2. Spread the chickpeas, bell pepper, courgette, and red onion on the tray. Drizzle with 2 tablespoons of olive oil. Sprinkle with smoked paprika, cumin, salt, and pepper. Toss to coat evenly.
  3. Roast for 25 minutes, stirring halfway through, until the chickpeas are crisp and the vegetables are tender and lightly charred at the edges.
  4. While the vegetables roast, prepare the tahini dressing: whisk together the tahini, lemon juice, grated garlic, remaining 1 tablespoon of olive oil, and water until smooth. Add more water if needed to reach a drizzling consistency.
  5. Assemble the bowls: place the quinoa or brown rice as a base, add the rocket or mixed greens, and top with the roasted chickpeas and vegetables.
  6. Drizzle generously with the tahini dressing. Garnish with fresh parsley and serve immediately.

Tip. To reduce hand strain, purchase pre-cut vegetables and use canned chickpeas exclusively. The roasting tray can be lined with foil for easier cleanup. Leftover roasted vegetables and chickpeas store well separately and can be assembled into fresh bowls over 2 to 3 days.

Nutrition (per serving). Protein: 19 g | Fibre: 13 g | Vitamin C: 145 mg (160% DV) | Vitamin E: 5 mg | Iron: 5 mg

3

Ginger-Miso Soba Noodle Bowl with Edamame

20 minutes | 2 servings | Very Easy | ~420 kcal per serving

This Japanese-inspired noodle bowl is one of the fastest complete meals in this guide. Buckwheat soba noodles are naturally gluten-free (when labelled 100% buckwheat) and provide rutin, a flavonoid with direct anti-inflammatory properties. The miso-ginger dressing combines fermented and anti-inflammatory ingredients in one sauce, while edamame delivers complete plant protein with isoflavones that modulate immune function.

Why it helps: Miso paste is a fermented food that provides both probiotic organisms and enzymes that support nutrient absorption. Ginger contains gingerols and shogaols that inhibit prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis through a mechanism similar to NSAIDs. Buckwheat provides rutin, which has been shown to reduce TNF-α and IL-6 levels in inflammatory models. Edamame isoflavones (genistein, daidzein) have documented immunomodulatory effects that may benefit autoimmune conditions.

Ingredients

  • 200 g soba noodles (100% buckwheat for gluten-free)
  • 1 cup shelled edamame (frozen is fine)
  • 1 large carrot, julienned or grated
  • 1 cup red cabbage, thinly sliced
  • ½ cucumber, sliced into thin half-moons
  • 2 spring onions, sliced
  • 1 tbsp sesame seeds

For the dressing:

  • 2 tbsp white miso paste
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, finely grated
  • 1 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tbsp sesame oil
  • 1 tbsp tamari or soy sauce
  • 1 tsp maple syrup
  • 2 tbsp warm water

Method

  1. Bring a large pot of water to the boil. Cook the soba noodles according to the packet instructions (usually 4 to 5 minutes). Add the frozen edamame to the pot during the last 2 minutes of cooking.
  2. While the noodles cook, prepare the dressing: whisk together the miso paste, grated ginger, rice vinegar, sesame oil, tamari, maple syrup, and warm water until smooth.
  3. Prepare the vegetables: julienne or grate the carrot, slice the red cabbage thinly, and cut the cucumber into half-moons. Use pre-cut vegetables if hand dexterity is limited.
  4. Drain the noodles and edamame. Rinse briefly under cold water to stop the cooking and prevent sticking.
  5. Divide the noodles between two bowls. Arrange the carrot, red cabbage, cucumber, and edamame on top.
  6. Pour the miso-ginger dressing over each bowl. Garnish with spring onions and sesame seeds. Serve immediately.

Tip. The dressing can be made in a larger batch and stored in the refrigerator for up to one week, eliminating the most hand-intensive step on subsequent preparations. If hand strength is limited, use kitchen scissors to cut the spring onions instead of a knife.

Nutrition (per serving). Protein: 22 g | Fibre: 8 g | Manganese: 1.8 mg (78% DV) | Magnesium: 95 mg | Folate: 210 µg

Warm Sweet Potato & Black Bean Tacos with Avocado Crema

30 minutes | 2 servings (3 tacos each) | Easy | ~480 kcal per serving

These vibrant tacos combine two of the most nutrient-dense anti-inflammatory ingredients available to vegetarians: sweet potatoes and black beans. Sweet potatoes are exceptionally rich in beta-carotene, a precursor to vitamin A that supports immune tolerance, while black beans provide anthocyanins (from their dark pigment) with potent antioxidant activity. The avocado crema adds healthy monounsaturated fats that improve the absorption of fat-soluble antioxidants.

Why it helps: Sweet potatoes contain beta-carotene, sporamins (unique anti-inflammatory proteins), and vitamin C. Black beans are among the highest antioxidant-scoring legumes due to their anthocyanin content, which inhibits NF-κB signalling. Avocado provides glutathione, the body’s master antioxidant, and oleic acid that reduces CRP levels. Lime juice provides vitamin C, which supports collagen synthesis and iron absorption from plant sources.

Ingredients

  • 1 large sweet potato, peeled and diced into 1 cm cubes
  • 1 can (400 g) black beans, drained and rinsed
  • 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • ½ tsp chilli powder (reduce if sensitive)
  • 6 small corn or whole wheat tortillas
  • ½ cup red cabbage, finely shredded
  • Fresh coriander (cilantro) to garnish

For the avocado crema:

  • 1 ripe avocado
  • Juice of 1 lime
  • 2 tbsp plain yoghurt (dairy or plant-based)
  • 1 small clove garlic
  • Pinch of salt

Method

  1. Preheat the oven to 200°C (400°F). Toss the diced sweet potato with olive oil, cumin, smoked paprika, chilli powder, and a pinch of salt. Spread on a lined baking tray in a single layer.
  2. Roast for 20 to 25 minutes, turning once halfway through, until the sweet potato is tender and lightly caramelised at the edges.
  3. During the last 5 minutes of roasting, add the drained black beans to the tray alongside the sweet potato to warm them through.
  4. While the sweet potato roasts, prepare the avocado crema: blend or mash together the avocado, lime juice, yoghurt, garlic, and salt until smooth.
  5. Warm the tortillas in a dry pan for 30 seconds per side, or wrap in foil and place in the oven for the last 3 minutes of the sweet potato roasting time.
  6. Assemble the tacos: place the roasted sweet potato and black beans in each tortilla, top with shredded red cabbage, a generous spoonful of avocado crema, and fresh coriander.

Tip. If hand dexterity is a concern, prepare this as a bowl instead of individual tacos, layer the tortilla pieces, sweet potato, beans, and toppings in a bowl for easier eating. The avocado crema can be mashed roughly with a fork rather than blended if a blender is difficult to manage.

Nutrition (per serving). Protein: 17 g | Fibre: 18 g | Beta-carotene: 11,500 µg | Vitamin C: 42 mg | Potassium: 980 mg

Walnut & Berry Power Salad with Flaxseed Vinaigrette

15 minutes | 2 servings | Very Easy, No Cooking | ~400 kcal per serving

This no-cook salad is the simplest complete meal in this collection, designed specifically for days when RA symptoms make cooking difficult. Walnuts are the only tree nut with a significant omega-3 content (as alpha-linolenic acid), and blueberries are among the most polyphenol-rich fruits available. The flaxseed vinaigrette provides additional omega-3s in a format that enhances the absorption of fat-soluble nutrients from the salad vegetables. Suggested Weekly Meal Plan The following table provides a sample week incorporating these five meals alongside simple anti-inflammatory breakfast and snack suggestions. This is a flexible guide, adjust based on your appetite, symptoms, and personal preferences.

Why it helps: Walnuts provide ALA omega-3 fatty acids that are converted to EPA and DHA, directly opposing the arachidonic acid inflammatory cascade active in RA joints. Blueberry anthocyanins and pterostilbene have been shown to reduce TNF-α and IL-1β production. Pumpkin seeds (pepitas) are one of the richest dietary sources of zinc, which is frequently depleted in RA and is essential for immune regulation. The flaxseed oil in the vinaigrette provides additional ALA in its most bioavailable form.

Ingredients

  • 4 cups mixed salad greens (spinach, rocket, romaine)
  • ½ cup blueberries
  • ½ cup strawberries, quartered
  • ¼ cup walnuts, roughly chopped
  • 2 tbsp pumpkin seeds (pepitas)
  • ¼ cup crumbled feta or goat cheese (or plant-based alternative)
  • ¼ cup cooked quinoa (optional, for added protein)
  • ½ avocado, sliced

For the flaxseed vinaigrette:

  • 2 tbsp flaxseed oil (cold-pressed)
  • 1 tbsp apple cider vinegar
  • 1 tsp Dijon mustard
  • 1 tsp honey or maple syrup
  • ¼ tsp ground turmeric
  • Pinch of salt and black pepper

Method

  1. Prepare the flaxseed vinaigrette first: whisk together the flaxseed oil, apple cider vinegar, Dijon mustard, honey, turmeric, salt, and pepper in a small jar or bowl until emulsified.
  2. Place the mixed salad greens in a large bowl or divide between two plates.
  3. Arrange the blueberries, strawberries, sliced avocado, and quinoa (if using) over the greens.
  4. Scatter the chopped walnuts, pumpkin seeds, and crumbled cheese on top.
  5. Drizzle the flaxseed vinaigrette over the salad just before serving.
  6. Toss gently and serve immediately. Do not store with the dressing applied, as the greens will wilt.

Tip. Purchase pre-washed salad greens and pre-sliced strawberries to eliminate all cutting. The vinaigrette can be made in a batch at the start of the week and shaken before each use. Store flaxseed oil in the refrigerator to prevent oxidation of its omega-3 content. Never heat flaxseed oil.

Nutrition (per serving). Protein: 14 g | Fibre: 10 g | ALA Omega-3: 7.2 g | Zinc: 3.4 mg | Vitamin E: 6 mg

Important Note. These recipes are intended to complement, not replace, your prescribed medical treatment for rheumatoid arthritis. Dietary changes can support overall health and may help reduce systemic inflammation, but they should be discussed with your rheumatologist or dietitian. This is particularly important if you have food allergies, are on methotrexate or other immunosuppressive medications (note the importance of adequate folate intake), or have other dietary restrictions. Vegetarian diets may require B12 supplementation, discuss this with your healthcare provider.
