For patients · Medication guide

Anifrolumab

Saphnelo, anti-IFNAR1, type I interferon receptor antagonist

Are you a clinician? See the clinical version of this page.

The short versionA lupus medicine, given as a drip or a weekly injection, added to your usual treatment to lower lupus activity and flares.
How you take it

Given either as a drip into a vein every 4 weeks, or as a weekly injection under the skin.

  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (IV): A drip into a vein over about 30 minutes, every 4 weeks.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (subcutaneous): A weekly injection under the skin.

What to know

How it is used in lupus

Anifrolumab is added on top of the usual lupus medicines to lower disease activity and reduce flares. It works by blocking the type I interferon signal, which is overactive in many people with lupus.

Common side effects

The more common effects include upper respiratory infections, bronchitis, shingles (herpes zoster) and cold sores, and reactions during an infusion if given by drip.

Before starting

Your team will check you do not have an active infection and will bring your vaccinations up to date first. Live vaccines are avoided while on treatment.

Monitoring

Watch for signs of infection, including shingles, and tell your team. Infusion reactions are watched for during and after a drip.

What to watch for

  • Signs of infection, such as fever, or a painful blistering rash (shingles). Contact your team.
  • A reaction during or after an infusion, such as rash, wheeze, or feeling faint.
  • New cold sores or other infections while on treatment.
