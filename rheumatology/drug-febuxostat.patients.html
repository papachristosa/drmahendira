Febuxostat

febuxostat, Uloric

Are you a clinician? See the clinical version of this page.

The short versionFebuxostat is another daily medicine that lowers uric acid in gout. It is usually used when allopurinol is not suitable, and like allopurinol it is adjusted to a target level.
How you take it

Taken as a tablet once a day, with or without food, and it can be taken with antacids. As with other uric-acid-lowering medicines, gout can flare when you start, so you may be given another medicine to prevent flares at first. It is generally used when allopurinol has not worked well enough or is not suitable.

  • Gout (urate lowering): Usually started at the lower dose and increased if your uric acid is still high after a couple of weeks.

Tests while you take it

You may have blood tests for uric acid and liver function. Because of a heart-safety concern, tell your team about any chest pain, shortness of breath, weakness, or a fast or irregular heartbeat.

What to know

How it is used in gout

Febuxostat is a second-line choice, often used if allopurinol causes problems or does not lower uric acid enough. It works in a similar way and is taken once a day.

Monitoring

Your uric acid is checked to guide the dose to target. If you have heart disease, your team weighs this up and may prefer a different medicine.

Common effects

Common effects can include liver blood-test changes, nausea, joint aches, and rash. As with any uric-acid-lowering medicine, attacks can happen more at first.

Important cautions

If you have heart problems, tell your team, as there have been concerns about heart risks with this medicine. Report any rash or feeling unwell.

What to watch for

  • Chest pain, breathlessness, or symptoms of a heart problem, which should be assessed promptly given cardiovascular safety concerns.
  • A rash or feeling unwell after starting.
  • More frequent attacks in the first weeks, which is expected while your levels settle.
