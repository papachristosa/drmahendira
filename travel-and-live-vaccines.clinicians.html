Travel, live vaccines, and yellow fever: a clinician summary

A practical summary for counselling patients on immunosuppressive or immunomodulatory therapy who are planning international travel. The recurring issue is yellow fever: a live-attenuated vaccine that is contraindicated in significant immunosuppression, yet is the one travel vaccine that also functions as a legal entry requirement under the International Health Regulations.

Patient versionVaccinations
iBottom line: identify travel plans early. Live vaccines, including yellow fever (YF-17D), are contraindicated on meaningful immunosuppression. When a certificate is required and the patient cannot be vaccinated, the practical route is a documented medical exemption on the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis, issued at a designated centre. This does not remove disease risk in a true transmission zone.

1. The core issue

Live-attenuated vaccines carry a risk of vaccine-associated infection in immunosuppressed hosts and are generally contraindicated during significant immunosuppression. For travel, the live vaccines to flag are yellow fever (YF-17D), the oral Ty21a typhoid vaccine, and, less commonly requested, live-attenuated influenza (nasal spray), MMR, varicella and BCG. Most other travel vaccines, including hepatitis A and B, injectable typhoid, rabies, Japanese encephalitis, meningococcal conjugate and the inactivated oral cholera vaccine available in Canada, are non-live and safe to administer. The graded recommendations behind this, and the medicine-specific timing points, are set out on the clinic's vaccinations page.

2. Yellow fever specifics

Yellow fever transmission risk is confined to parts of sub-Saharan Africa and tropical South America. Public health guidance lists immunosuppressive and immunomodulatory therapy, solid organ transplantation, malignancy, primary immunodeficiency and symptomatic HIV among the vaccine's contraindications. A single dose is now taken to confer lifelong immunity for most people, and certificate validity is recorded as the lifetime of the person vaccinated; boosters are no longer routinely required. In Canada the vaccine, and the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis, can only be issued at centres designated by the Public Health Agency of Canada.

Two distinct requirement types exist. Some states require proof of vaccination from all arriving travellers; others require it only for travellers arriving from, or having transited, a country with risk of transmission. This is why the full itinerary, including transit points, must be reviewed rather than the final destination alone.

3. The medical exemption pathway

Where the vaccine is contraindicated but a certificate would otherwise be required, a clinician at a designated centre can document a medical contraindication on the certificate. Many states accept a waiver, but acceptance is at the discretion of the destination and is not guaranteed, so it should be confirmed for the specific route, ideally with the relevant embassy or consulate. Counsel patients that the waiver addresses the entry requirement only. For an itinerary that genuinely enters a transmission zone, the unmet protection need is a separate and sometimes decisive consideration, and rethinking the route may be the safer advice.

4. Timing around immunomodulators

Where live vaccination is being contemplated, for example in a patient not yet started on treatment, defined intervals apply before and after immunosuppressive therapy, and the response can be blunted by ongoing treatment. This is specialised travel-medicine territory that interacts with disease control; the practical approach is early referral to a travel medicine or designated yellow fever centre, with the vaccination decision and any hold of therapy coordinated with the treating rheumatologist rather than actioned in isolation.

5. Checking requirements

Direct patients, and check yourself, using current official sources rather than static lists: the Government of Canada destination advice pages (Health tab, pre-travel vaccinations) for each country including transit points; the destination's embassy or consulate for anything unusual; and, for a country-by-country reference, the US CDC Yellow Book requirements by country. Advise a travel-health assessment about six weeks before departure.
