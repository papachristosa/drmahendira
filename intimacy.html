Intimacy, relationships, and rheumatic disease

Rheumatic disease can affect intimacy and relationships, and it is a common concern that often goes unspoken. This page covers how, some practical things that can help, and why it is worth raising with your team. It is general information, written plainly and without assumptions about your situation.

1. A normal part of life, often left unspoken

Intimacy matters to quality of life, and many people with a rheumatic disease notice it is affected at some point, yet it rarely gets mentioned in appointments. There is no reason to feel awkward about it. It is a common, understandable concern, and it is worth talking about, both with your partner and, where it helps, with your care team.

2. How rheumatic disease can affect intimacy

Several things can play a part. Pain and stiffness can make some positions uncomfortable. Fatigue can lower energy and desire. Some conditions, such as Sjogren's, cause dryness that makes sex uncomfortable. Changes to how your body looks or feels, from rashes, joint changes, or medicines like steroids, can affect confidence and body image. Low mood can reduce interest. And occasionally a medicine itself can affect libido or function. Knowing that these are physical, understandable effects, not something you are doing wrong, often takes some of the pressure off.

3. Practical things that can help

Small adjustments make a difference. Timing intimacy for when stiffness and energy are at their best, and using your usual pain relief beforehand, can help. Experimenting with positions and using pillows for support can ease sore joints. For dryness, lubricants are simple and effective. And treating the things underneath, pain, fatigue, mood, and dryness, often helps more broadly. Above all, talking openly with your partner about what feels good and what does not takes the guesswork out.

4. It is okay to raise it with your team

If intimacy is being affected, your rheumatology team or family doctor can help with the medical parts, such as managing dryness or pain, reviewing a medicine that might be contributing, or referring you on to someone who specialises in this. Relationships can also come under strain when one person has a chronic illness, and support for that is available too. You are allowed to ask, and asking is a sign of looking after your whole self.
