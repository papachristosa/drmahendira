Osteoarthritis Clinic

Non-surgical osteoarthritis care, and an injection service worth trying first.

An interdisciplinary, non-surgical approach to osteoarthritis, including a joint-injection service. Each injection has its own guide: what it is, what the evidence says, what to expect, and how to prepare.

Conditions we treat

The clinic assesses and treats a range of common joint, tendon, and nerve problems. Each guide explains the condition, and is honest about where an injection does and does not help.

Osteoarthritis

Osteoarthritis

Knee osteoarthritis

The most common joint we treat. Aches, stiffens, and swells with use.

Hip osteoarthritis

Wear of the hip joint, usually felt in the groin or thigh.

Shoulder osteoarthritis (glenohumeral)

Hand osteoarthritis

Tendon & soft tissue

Trigger finger

A finger that catches, clicks, or locks. Injection usually works well.

Read guideTendon & soft tissue

De Quervain's tenosynovitis

Painful tendons on the thumb side of the wrist. Injection often helps.

Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis)

Outer-elbow tendon pain. Here the injection evidence is cautionary.

Golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis)

Inner-elbow tendon pain. Like tennis elbow, exercise is the mainstay.

iAfter any injection, the injection aftercare guide covers what to do in the days that follow.

For osteoarthritis that may need surgery, we co-manage with orthopaedic surgery at St. Michael's, including rapid-access consults with Dr. Amir Khoshbin, Chief of Orthopaedic Surgery, whose focus is hip and knee joint replacement. A conservative-management pathway (education, exercise, and load management), including evidence-based group programs such as GLA:D Canada, sits alongside the injection service.
