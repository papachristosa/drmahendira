Patient nutrition guide · For Patients with Rheumatoid Arthritis

5 Easy Recipes to

Improve Your Gut Microbiome. By Dr. D. Mahendira.

All nutrition guidesAll patient guides

Research shows that patients with rheumatoid arthritis have reduced gut microbial diversity compared to healthy individuals. Improving your microbiome through diet may help reduce inflammation, support immune regulation, and complement your medical treatment. These five recipes are designed to be simple to prepare, gentle on the joints, and rich in both probiotic and prebiotic ingredients.

Each recipe includes step-by-step instructions, an ingredient list, and an explanation of how it supports your gut health. All recipes are designed with joint-friendly preparation methods.

1

Golden Turmeric Sauerkraut Soup

20 minutes | 4 servings | Very Easy

This warming soup combines the potent anti-inflammatory properties of turmeric and ginger with the probiotic richness of sauerkraut. Fermented foods like sauerkraut introduce beneficial Lactobacillus bacteria directly into the gut, while turmeric’s active compound curcumin has been shown to modulate the gut microbiome and reduce inflammatory cytokines relevant to RA.

Why it helps: Sauerkraut provides live Lactobacillus and Leuconostoc strains that colonise the gut and produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs). Turmeric and ginger reduce NF-κB-mediated inflammation. The olive oil provides monounsaturated fats that support gut barrier integrity.

Ingredients

  • 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, grated
  • 1 tbsp ground turmeric (or 2 tbsp fresh, grated)
  • 4 cups low-sodium vegetable broth
  • 1 cup unpasteurised sauerkraut (with brine)
  • ½ tsp black pepper (enhances curcumin absorption)
  • Salt to taste

Method

  1. Heat olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the diced onion and cook for 3 to 4 minutes until softened.
  2. Add garlic and ginger. Stir for 1 minute until fragrant. Do not let the garlic burn.
  3. Add turmeric and black pepper. Stir to coat the onion mixture evenly for about 30 seconds.
  4. Pour in the vegetable broth. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 10 minutes.
  5. Remove from heat and allow to cool for 2 to 3 minutes. This protects the live cultures in the sauerkraut.
  6. Stir in the sauerkraut (with its brine) once the soup has cooled slightly. Serve warm, not hot.

Tip. Never boil the sauerkraut, heat kills the live probiotic cultures. Always add it after removing the soup from the heat. Look for sauerkraut labelled “unpasteurised” or “raw” in the refrigerated section.

2

Overnight Prebiotic Oats with Kefir

5 minutes prep + overnight | 1 serving | Very Easy (No Cooking)

This no-cook breakfast requires no hand strength or prolonged preparation, making it ideal for RA patients experiencing morning stiffness. Kefir is one of the most potent probiotic foods available, containing up to 61 different microbial strains. Combined with prebiotic-rich oats and ground flaxseed, this recipe both introduces and feeds beneficial gut bacteria.

Why it helps: Kefir provides a diverse range of probiotic strains (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, and beneficial yeasts) that have been shown to reduce inflammatory markers in autoimmune conditions. Oats contain beta-glucan, a prebiotic fibre that selectively feeds Bifidobacterium. Flaxseed provides omega-3 ALA and lignans that support gut barrier function.

Ingredients

  • ½ cup rolled oats (not instant)
  • ¾ cup plain kefir (full-fat preferred)
  • 1 tbsp ground flaxseed
  • 1 tbsp chia seeds
  • ½ cup mixed berries (blueberries, raspberries)
  • 1 tsp honey or maple syrup (optional)
  • Pinch of cinnamon

Method

  1. Add rolled oats, ground flaxseed, and chia seeds to a jar or container with a lid.
  2. Pour in the kefir and stir gently until everything is well combined.
  3. Add cinnamon and honey if using. Stir once more.
  4. Seal the container and place it in the refrigerator overnight (minimum 6 hours).
  5. In the morning, stir the oats. Add berries on top. Eat cold or at room temperature.
  6. If the consistency is too thick, add a splash of kefir or water and stir.

Tip. Prepare 2 to 3 jars at once to reduce the number of preparation days needed during the week. This is especially helpful during RA flare periods when hand function is limited. Use a wide-mouth jar for easier stirring.

3

Anti-Inflammatory Miso Salmon Bowl

25 minutes | 2 servings | Easy

This bowl combines fermented miso paste with omega-3-rich salmon over a bed of fibre-rich brown rice and vegetables. Miso is a traditional Japanese fermented food that introduces Aspergillus oryzae and Lactobacillus strains into the gut, while the high omega-3 content of salmon directly counters the inflammatory pathways active in RA.

Why it helps: Miso provides both probiotic organisms and enzymes that aid nutrient absorption. Salmon is one of the richest dietary sources of EPA and DHA omega-3 fatty acids, which inhibit prostaglandin and leukotriene synthesis. Broccoli and edamame provide sulforaphane and prebiotic fibre that support microbial diversity.

Ingredients

  • 2 salmon fillets (approx. 150 g each)
  • 2 tbsp white miso paste
  • 1 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tsp sesame oil
  • 1½ cups cooked brown rice
  • 1 cup steamed broccoli florets
  • ½ cup shelled edamame
  • 1 tbsp sesame seeds
  • ½ avocado, sliced

Method

  1. Preheat the oven to 200°C (400°F). Line a baking tray with parchment paper.
  2. In a small bowl, whisk together the miso paste, rice vinegar, and sesame oil until smooth.
  3. Place salmon fillets on the tray. Spread the miso glaze evenly over the top of each fillet.
  4. Bake for 12 to 15 minutes until the salmon is cooked through and the glaze is lightly caramelised.
  5. While salmon bakes, steam the broccoli and warm the edamame. Prepare the brown rice.
  6. Assemble bowls: rice on the base, broccoli and edamame alongside, salmon on top. Add avocado and sesame seeds.

Tip. White (shiro) miso is milder and contains more active cultures than darker varieties. When mixing the glaze, keep the miso paste below 50°C to preserve some live cultures in the uncooked portions. The baked portion will lose cultures but retains the enzymatic and nutritional benefits.

4

Berry Kombucha Smoothie

5 minutes | 1 serving | Very Easy (No Cooking)

This smoothie is an excellent option for days when RA symptoms make meal preparation difficult. Kombucha provides a diverse probiotic profile including Gluconacetobacter and Saccharomyces strains, while the berry and spinach base delivers polyphenols and prebiotic fibre that selectively promote the growth of anti-inflammatory gut bacteria.

Why it helps: Kombucha provides a diverse community of acetic acid bacteria and yeasts that produce organic acids supporting gut pH balance. Blueberry and pomegranate polyphenols are metabolised by gut bacteria into urolithins, which have demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects in RA models. Spinach provides folate and magnesium, both commonly depleted in RA patients on methotrexate.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup plain or ginger kombucha (unflavoured preferred)
  • ½ cup frozen blueberries
  • ½ frozen banana
  • 1 handful baby spinach (approx. 30 g)
  • 2 tbsp pomegranate seeds
  • 1 tbsp almond butter
  • 1 tsp ground flaxseed

Method

  1. Add the kombucha to the blender first (liquid on the bottom prevents jamming).
  2. Add the frozen blueberries, frozen banana, and baby spinach on top of the liquid.
  3. Add the almond butter and ground flaxseed.
  4. Blend on medium speed for 30 to 45 seconds until smooth. Do not over-blend.
  5. Pour into a glass. Top with pomegranate seeds for added polyphenols and crunch.
  6. Drink immediately for maximum probiotic benefit. Do not heat or store for later.

Tip. Choose kombucha with minimal added sugar (less than 5 g per serving). Avoid blending at high speed for too long, as excess heat and aeration can reduce live culture counts. If grip strength is limited, use a lightweight cup with a handle.

5

One-Pan Mediterranean Lentil Bake

35 minutes | 4 servings | Easy

This one-pan dish minimises preparation effort while maximising prebiotic and anti-inflammatory content. Lentils are among the richest dietary sources of prebiotic fibre, feeding Bifidobacterium and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, a keystone species frequently depleted in RA patients. The dish is topped with a yoghurt drizzle that adds a final probiotic layer. Quick Reference: Key Microbiome Foods for RA The table below summarises the most important categories of microbiome-supporting foods featured in these recipes and their mechanism of benefit in rheumatoid arthritis.

Why it helps: Lentils provide resistant starch and prebiotic galacto-oligosaccharides that selectively feed anti-inflammatory gut bacteria. F. prausnitzii produces butyrate, a SCFA that strengthens the gut barrier and reduces systemic inflammation. The garlic and onion provide additional prebiotic fructo-oligosaccharides. The yoghurt topping contributes Lactobacillus and Streptococcus thermophilus.

Ingredients

  • 1 can (400 g) green or brown lentils, drained and rinsed
  • 1 can (400 g) diced tomatoes
  • 1 red bell pepper, chopped (pre-cut to reduce hand strain)
  • 1 small red onion, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 tsp cumin, 1 tsp smoked paprika, ½ tsp oregano
  • ¼ cup plain live-culture yoghurt (for drizzle)
  • Fresh parsley or coriander to garnish

Method

  1. Preheat the oven to 190°C (375°F). Use a large oven-safe pan or baking dish.
  2. Toss the lentils, diced tomatoes, bell pepper, red onion, and garlic in the pan.
  3. Drizzle with olive oil. Add the cumin, smoked paprika, oregano, and a pinch of salt. Stir to combine.
  4. Cover with foil and bake for 25 minutes until the vegetables are tender and the sauce has thickened.
  5. Remove from oven. Let it cool for 3 to 4 minutes before serving.
  6. Drizzle each serving with a tablespoon of live-culture yoghurt and garnish with fresh herbs.

Tip. Use canned lentils instead of dried to eliminate the soaking and boiling step entirely. Pre-cut vegetables are available in most grocery stores and are worth the extra cost to reduce hand strain. The yoghurt should be added after baking to preserve live cultures.

Important Note. These recipes are intended to complement, not replace, your prescribed medical treatment for rheumatoid arthritis. Dietary changes can support your overall health and may help reduce inflammation, but they should be discussed with your rheumatologist or dietitian, particularly if you have food allergies, are on methotrexate or other immunosuppressive medications, or have other dietary restrictions.
