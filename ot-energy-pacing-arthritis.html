Energy and pacing

Fatigue is one of the most common and most underestimated parts of inflammatory arthritis. Planning and pacing your day, rather than pushing through, helps you keep energy for what matters.

All patient guidesAll patient guides

Why fatigue happens

Fatigue in inflammatory arthritis is more than ordinary tiredness. Active inflammation, disturbed sleep, pain, low mood, and sometimes anaemia all feed into it. It is real, it is common, and it responds better to planning than to pushing through.

The four P's

Occupational therapists often teach four simple habits:

  • Prioritise. Decide what actually matters today and let the rest wait. Not everything needs doing, and not all by you.
  • Pace. Break tasks into smaller pieces, alternate heavy and light jobs, and rest before you are exhausted rather than after.
  • Plan. Put demanding tasks in the part of the day you have most energy, and build rest into the plan on purpose.
  • Position. Set things up so you can sit rather than stand, keep what you use often within reach, and avoid awkward postures that cost energy.

A simple pacing plan

For a few days, jot down what you do and how tired each task leaves you. Patterns show up fast: a heavy morning that wipes out the afternoon, or three big jobs stacked together. Then spread the heavy tasks across the week, put a rest between them, and stop a task while you still have a little left rather than running to empty. Pacing is not about doing less forever, it is about doing it in a way you can repeat tomorrow.

Building rest into the day

  • Short, regular rests beat one long collapse at the end of the day.
  • Sit for tasks you would normally stand for, such as food prep, folding, or showering with a shower stool.
  • Accept and organise help: a shared list makes it easier for others to step in.

Pacing through a flare

In a flare, scale back for a short while rather than stopping altogether. Protect the essentials, drop or delegate the rest, and build activity back up gradually as the flare settles. Keeping some gentle movement going, within comfort, usually helps more than complete rest.

When to tell your team

Tell your team if fatigue is severe, is suddenly worse, or comes with other new symptoms. Active disease and treatable causes such as anaemia or an underactive thyroid can look like ordinary tiredness, and are worth checking.
