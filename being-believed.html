General Rheumatology · Being believed

Being believed, and your journey to a diagnosis

Many people with a rheumatic disease describe not being believed at some point, especially when symptoms are invisible or come and go. If that has been your experience, you are not alone, and it was not your fault. This page is about why it happens and what helps now.

Symptom trackersCommon questions
iYour symptoms are information, not complaints. The clearer the picture you can give of what you are experiencing, the easier it is for your team to act on it.

1. If you have felt dismissed, you are not alone

Feeling disbelieved is one of the most common things patients tell researchers they have been through, particularly in conditions where the symptoms are invisible, fluctuate, or do not show up on early tests. Some people are told their symptoms are stress, or in their head, sometimes for years. That experience is real, and it can leave a lasting mark on how you feel about seeking care. It is not a reflection on you, and it does not mean your symptoms were not real.

2. Why diagnosis can take so long

Rheumatic diseases are genuinely hard to pin down early. Symptoms often start vague and shifting, blood tests and scans are not always clear at the beginning, and several conditions can look like more ordinary problems at first. For some inflammatory conditions the time from first symptoms to diagnosis is measured in years, not months. None of that makes the delay less frustrating, but it does mean it is usually a reflection of how the diseases behave, not of you being difficult or imagining things.

3. What helps now

The single most useful thing you can do is keep a clear record of your symptoms over time, and describe the pattern rather than only the worst day: when things are better or worse, what you can and cannot do, and how it affects your work, sleep, and mood. Our symptom trackers are built for exactly this and produce a printable summary for your appointment. A concrete record gives your team something solid to work from and makes it much easier for you to be heard.

4. If trust has been damaged

If a difficult path to diagnosis has dented your confidence in doctors, it is completely understandable, and it is fine to say so. Being under specialist care is a chance for a fresh start, where the focus shifts to getting the picture clear and the treatment right. You deserve to be believed, and to be followed until things are sorted out. If something you are told does not sit right, it is always reasonable to ask questions or to seek a second opinion.
